WHB

Art Director

William Henry Blackburn (1918–1995), husband to Dhanlaxmi and father to Munindra Nigel Blackburn, was a British art director, draughtsman, calligrapher, and exhibition designer whose career moved between wartime service, government information work in India, and decades of commercial advertising. After the Second World War he became Art Director at D.J. Keymer & Co. Ltd. in Bombay, and later at the agency's head office in Calcutta. He designed and supervised exhibition stands and trade-fair displays as readily as he directed print campaigns, occupying a rare space where fine art, lettering, and commercial design met in a single working life.

D.J. Keymer, Bombay & Calcutta

D.J. Keymer & Co. Ltd. was one of the leading British advertising agencies in India, with studios in Bombay and Calcutta that shaped mid-century commercial art across the subcontinent. William spent the heart of his postwar career there as Art Director, before his transfer to the Calcutta head office.

The Calcutta office brought together an extraordinary circle of visual talent. He worked alongside Annada Munshi, the art director regarded as the father of Indian commercial art, who shaped a generation of designers in layout, calligraphy, and Swadeshi visual identity. D. K. Gupta, Keymer manager and founder of Signet Press, was a close colleague; so too was Satyajit Ray, who joined the agency as a junior visualiser in 1943 and would go on to become one of the greatest filmmakers of the twentieth century. William was the only white Englishman in the staff photograph of the Calcutta team—a measure of how deeply he had made India his professional home. The agency later became part of the lineage that led to Ogilvy Benson & Mather in India.

Paintings

William was not simply a painter in the narrow sense. Alongside decades of commercial design, he maintained a substantial body of paintings and drawings across both England and India from the 1940s onwards. Indian subjects—figures, landscapes, and scenes from daily life—recur throughout the archive alongside work made at home. The same sensibility that directed campaigns for major industrial clients also produced oil paintings, sketches, and calligraphic pieces. He worked alongside the artist Harry Baines during his years in India. Both served with the Royal Engineers and were seconded to the Government of India's exhibitions and information work; their careers followed strikingly parallel paths, and their painting styles were notably similar. Much of this work has remained outside public view since his death, preserved within the family collection.

Draughtsman and WW2 Engineer

From 1939 to 1942 William served with the King's Royal Rifle Corps and Royal Engineers in the United Kingdom. From 1942 to 1945 he was in India, attached to the Royal Engineers and the Government of India's Information and Broadcasting Department. His wartime work extended well beyond routine military duty into exhibition and publicity design, work in New Delhi and Lahore, and—earlier in the war—conducting a School of Art for the Services under South East Army Command at Oxford. He was also a skilled calligrapher and lettering artist, producing formal inscriptions, layout designs, and bespoke text-based works with the same precision he brought to technical draughtsmanship. He built bridges during his Royal Engineers service, and spoke of that work throughout his life.

Royal Engineers

William's Royal Engineers service in India placed him at the intersection of military structure and visual communication. From 1939 to 1942 he served with the King's Royal Rifle Corps and Royal Engineers in the United Kingdom; from 1942 to 1945 he was in India, detached to the Royal Engineers and attached to the Government of India's Information and Broadcasting Department under Field Marshal Claude Auchinleck.

For William, engineering was never separate from visual work. The precision demanded of military planning and exhibition display would later reappear in his trade-fair maquettes, pavilion models, and the meticulous draughtsmanship that runs through the archive. The Royal Engineers period sits at the root of a career that united structure, design, and image—long before he became Art Director at D.J. Keymer in Bombay and Calcutta.

Love of India

From 1942 to 1945, William Henry Blackburn served in India with the Royal Engineers, attached to the Government of India's Information and Broadcasting Department during the wider Burma campaign against Japan. His archive includes a signed photograph of Field Marshal Sir Claude John Eyre Auchinleck, dated 21 March 1944. His wartime role placed him in the same specialist exhibition and information world as the artist Harry Baines, another Royal Engineers draughtsman seconded to government publicity and display work in India.

His responsibilities extended well beyond ordinary military service. He worked on exhibition and publicity design in New Delhi and Lahore, recruitment and public exhibitions, and earlier conducted a School of Art for the Services under South East Army Command at Oxford. Family recollection also records that William spoke of building bridges, a detail consistent with his Royal Engineers background.

India also shaped his personal life. He married Dhanlaxmi Mehta, a mathematician and professor, and together they later moved back to England, where they raised their son, Munindra Nigel Blackburn, known as Nigel, who went on to become a substantive orthopaedic consultant. William's Indian years also included the period of the Bengal Famine of 1943, placing his life and work within one of the most difficult chapters of wartime India.

Already an established artist before his time in India, William found there a profound source of subject matter, professional opportunity, and personal connection. India became one of the defining chapters of his life, expanding his work across painting, drawing, photography, exhibition design, calligraphy, advertising, and art direction.

Photographer

Photography sat alongside drawing and painting throughout William's career. He often used the camera as a working tool—gathering reference, studying form, and recording the life around him—yet many images in the archive stand as accomplished photographs in their own right, particularly from his years in India. Documentary portraits, street scenes, and studies of Indian life sit beside the commercial assignments of his professional life, reflecting the same eye for composition that shaped his exhibition design and art direction.

He worked on the first Pretty Polly advert—one of several points where his image-making crossed from personal observation into the history of British advertising. Much of his photographic work, like his paintings and drawings, has remained within the family collection since his death in 1995.
