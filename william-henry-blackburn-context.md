William Henry Blackburn – Reference Context for Website Build
Who William Henry Blackburn was

William Henry Blackburn was a British artist, draughtsman, calligrapher, lettering specialist, exhibition designer, and art director whose career spanned wartime military service, government information and exhibition work in India, major commercial advertising and display design, and a lifelong body of paintings, drawings, and visual work.

He was not simply a painter in the narrow sense. He appears to have worked across multiple overlapping disciplines, including fine art, technical draughtsmanship, architectural-style exhibition planning, lettering, calligraphy, commercial design, and art direction. His work and career reflect a rare combination of artistic sensitivity, technical precision, and high-level professional design practice.

The website being built for him should present him as a significant multidimensional visual artist and designer, not just as someone who made paintings.

Military and wartime context

Family material and surviving documents indicate that William Henry Blackburn served during the Second World War and was connected with the Royal Engineers.

An existing CV states:

1939–1942: U.K. – King’s Royal Rifle Corps / Royal Engineers
1942–1945: India – Detached, Royal Engineers; attached to the Government of India Information and Broadcasting Department

The same CV suggests that his wartime work was not limited to ordinary military duties. It included creative and technical responsibilities such as:

conducting a School of Art for the Services under South East Army Command at Oxford
exhibition and publicity design
work in New Delhi and Lahore
work connected to wartime information, recruitment, and public exhibitions

Family knowledge also states that he spoke of building bridges, which fits his Royal Engineers background, even if that specific duty is not detailed on the surviving CV.

His archive also includes a signed photograph of Claude Auchinleck, dated 21 March 1944, retained among his belongings from the India period, which strengthens the impression that he was operating in serious wartime administrative, exhibition, or information circles in India.

India years

India was a major part of William Henry Blackburn’s life and career.

He served there during the war and remained active there professionally afterward. His Indian years appear to have been central to his development as an artist, designer, and art director.

His surviving materials suggest a life and career shaped by:

British wartime India
public information and exhibition design
visual communication
architecture-like exhibition stand planning
Indian commercial art and advertising
Indian subjects in drawing and painting
professional agency work in Bombay and Calcutta

He also appears in a staff photograph associated with D.J. Keymer, a major advertising agency in India, where he is believed to have been the only white Englishman in the photographed team.

His archive includes many works from India, including paintings, drawings, exhibition models, and design material. India should be treated as one of the defining themes of the website.

D.J. Keymer, Benson, Ogilvy lineage

William Henry Blackburn worked for D.J. Keymer in India. D.J. Keymer later became part of the corporate lineage that ultimately led into Ogilvy Benson & Mather in India.

Pretty Polly

Family testimony establishes that William Henry Blackburn worked on the first Pretty Polly advert.

This is an important part of his legacy and should be retained in the project context even if it is not yet publicly documented online in detail.

The site can present this carefully as a family-attested fact if needed, especially in biography or archive notes.

Relationship to Harry Baines

There are strong parallels between William Henry Blackburn and Harry Baines, another draughtsman/artist associated with India and Royal Engineers-related work.

Based on the overlap in timing, location, role type, and visual/design context, it is considered highly plausible that they were working in the same narrow professional-military exhibition/design world in India, and possibly directly together.

This matters because it helps place Blackburn within a wider historical network of artist-draughtsmen working across military, governmental, and exhibition contexts in India during and after the war.

Suggested short biography for internal use

William Henry Blackburn was a British artist, draughtsman, calligrapher, exhibition designer, and art director whose career bridged wartime service, government information work in India, major commercial advertising accounts, and a substantial archive of paintings, drawings, and design work. During the Second World War he served in connection with the Royal Engineers and later worked in India in exhibition and publicity design. After the war he became Art Director at D.J. Keymer in Bombay and later Calcutta, working on major accounts including Shell, BOAC, Dunlop, ICI, Assam Oil, Philips, and others. He was also known for his lettering and calligraphy, and family testimony records that he worked on the first Pretty Polly advert. His surviving archive reveals a rare and compelling crossover between fine art, draughtsmanship, military-era design, exhibition planning, commercial art direction, and Indian visual culture.
